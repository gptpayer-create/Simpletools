Frequently Asked Questions

What is BMI and is it accurate?
BMI (Body Mass Index) is a simple measure of body fat based on height and weight. While useful as a screening tool, it has limitations — it doesn't distinguish between muscle and fat, and may not accurately reflect health for athletes, elderly, or different ethnic groups.
What is BMR?
BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) is the number of calories your body burns at complete rest — just to maintain vital functions like breathing, circulation, and cell production. This calculator uses the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, which is widely considered the most accurate formula.
What is TDEE?
TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) is the total calories you burn per day, accounting for your activity level. It's your BMR multiplied by an activity factor. Eating at TDEE maintains weight; eating below it creates a calorie deficit for weight loss.
Which ideal weight formula is most accurate?
No single formula is universally most accurate. The Devine formula (1974) is most widely used in clinical settings. The Robinson formula tends to be more generous for women. For practical health goals, the healthy BMI weight range (18.5–24.9) is generally more useful than any single ideal weight number.
What is Prime BMI?
Prime BMI is the ratio of your actual BMI to the upper boundary of the normal range (24.9). A Prime BMI of 1.0 means you're at the upper end of normal. Below 1.0 is normal/underweight, above 1.0 is overweight/obese.

BMI Calculator — Complete Guide

BMI (Body Mass Index) is a widely used measure that estimates body fat based on height and weight. It helps classify individuals as underweight, normal weight, overweight, or obese, providing a quick health screening metric.

⚖️ Quick health check

Calculate your BMI in seconds to get a basic understanding of whether your current weight is in a healthy range for your height.

🏋️ Track fitness progress

Use BMI as one of several metrics to monitor changes in your body composition as you progress through a fitness or weight management program.

👨‍⚕️ Doctor's reference

Healthcare providers use BMI to screen patients for potential weight-related health risks — though it's always used alongside other clinical measures.

📏 Understand your range

BMI under 18.5 is underweight, 18.5–24.9 is normal, 25–29.9 is overweight, and 30+ is obese — according to WHO classification standards.

BMI is calculated by dividing weight in kilograms by the square of height in meters (kg/m²). While BMI is a useful screening tool, it does not directly measure body fat and may not accurately reflect health for athletes, elderly individuals, or those with high muscle mass. Always consult a healthcare professional for a complete health assessment.
